---
title: "Sentence and paragraph rules"
description: "Rules for sentence length, structure, paragraph length, and punctuation."
---

## Sentence length

Maximum 25 words per sentence. Shorter is better. One main idea per sentence. Don't combine two actions or two concepts in one sentence.

Vary sentence length. Mix short sentences (around 8 words) with longer ones (up to 25 words). Uniform sentence length reads as robotic.

## Paragraph length

Keep paragraphs to two to three sentences for online documentation. Longer paragraphs create unnecessary cognitive load for readers who are scanning.

## If/then construction

Every "if" clause must include "then" in the predicate. No exceptions. This helps readers with lower English proficiency parse conditions correctly.

<CodeGroup>
```text Wrong
If you need updates, use Option A.
```

```text Right
If you need updates, then use Option A.
```
</CodeGroup>

## Capitalize after a colon

Capitalize when a full sentence follows a colon or hyphen. Use lowercase when what follows is a phrase or fragment.

<CodeGroup>
```text Full sentence after colon — capitalize
Note: This action is irreversible.
```

```text Fragment after colon — lowercase
Options: daily, weekly, or monthly.
```
</CodeGroup>

## Avoid "where" to connect clauses

Use "because," "since," or "so" instead.

<CodeGroup>
```text Wrong
Teams maintain duplicate codebases where maintenance costs double.
```

```text Right
Teams maintain duplicate codebases, so maintenance costs double.
```
</CodeGroup>

## Sentence starters

Occasionally start sentences with "And" or "But" when it creates natural flow. Avoid nested clauses. When a simpler structure works, use it.

<CodeGroup>
```text Nested — harder to read
The API, which requires authentication, which you set up in the previous step, returns a JSON response.
```

```text Simpler
The API requires authentication. Set it up in the previous step. It returns a JSON response.
```
</CodeGroup>

## No mechanical transitions

Never use: Additionally, Furthermore, Moreover, In conclusion, To summarise, In summary, As mentioned above, As previously stated.

## Abbreviations

Never use "e.g." Write "for example" in full. Never use "i.e." Write "that is" in full.

## Numbers and dates

Spell out numbers one through nine. Use numerals for 10 and above. Use ISO 8601 format for timestamps: `YYYY-MM-DDTHH:MM:SSZ`.

## Em-dashes

Use em-dashes when they improve clarity, emphasis, or flow. Use them for a sharp break in thought, immediate clarification, or a parenthetical remark that would be clunkier with commas or periods. Don't use them automatically or because they appear frequently in AI-generated text.

**When to use an em-dash:**
- You want to add a quick clarification mid-sentence.
- You want a deliberate pause or break in thought.
- You want stronger emphasis than a comma or parentheses provides.

**When to skip the em-dash:**
- A period is cleaner.
- A comma is enough.
- A colon gives a clearer lead-in.
- The sentence reads more naturally without it.

<CodeGroup>
```text Better with em-dash
The issue is clear—missing permissions caused the failure.
```

```text Better with a period
The issue is clear. Missing permissions caused the failure.
```

```text Better with a colon
The issue is clear: missing permissions caused the failure.
```
</CodeGroup>

All three are correct. Choose the one that reads best in context.

**The rule in plain terms:**
- Use em-dashes only when they improve clarity or emphasis.
- Don't use them automatically or because they are a common AI-writing pattern.
- Prefer periods, commas, or colons when they read more cleanly.
- Choose the punctuation that makes the sentence easiest to scan and understand.
