Good documentation doesn't just instruct. It gives users the story they need to tell their colleagues and managers about what they achieved. Storytelling principles help you do this without crossing into marketing territory.

## The Pixar framework

Pixar builds stories using three tools. Each applies directly to documentation.

### Share a unique perspective
Talk to pre-sales, marketing, and product teams who know customer stories. Use those stories as your foundation for documentation examples.

### Ask "what if" questions
These questions surface real problems and real solutions. They become the foundation of your scenario.

```text
What if you could track every deployment against your company strategy in real time?
What if you could find any event in your camera feed in seconds?
```

### Identify the obstacles
Name the specific barrier the product removes. When you show how the product removes a specific barrier, adoption increases because users recognize themselves in the problem.

## Modular content (the Lego principle)

Create self-contained content nuggets that can appear in multiple contexts:
- A documentation page
- A tutorial
- An in-app tooltip
- A support article

Write it once. Use it everywhere. Before writing, ask: where else could this information appear? What is the smallest useful unit of this content?

## Voice and tone by section

Your voice is constant. Your tone changes based on what the reader is doing and how they feel.

| Section | Reader's state | Tone to use |
|---|---|---|
| Introduction | Curious, open | Warm, direct, confident |
| Prerequisites | Practical, checking | Neutral, factual |
| Steps | Focused, working | Crisp, action-oriented |
| Expected output | Relieved or checking | Reassuring, affirming |
| Troubleshooting | Frustrated, stuck | Calm, specific, helpful |

## Four voice principles

**Plainspoken:** Write like a knowledgeable colleague, not a salesperson.

```text
Wrong: Unlock the power of GraphQL pagination to supercharge your data workflows.
Right: This guide shows you how to fetch all results from a paginated GraphQL API.
```

**Genuine:** Relate to the reader's challenge. Acknowledge what's hard. Don't pretend everything is easy.

**Clear over clever:** Never sacrifice clarity for a clever phrase. If a sentence is clever but confusing, rewrite it.

**Active voice always:** Makes clear who does what. Eliminates ambiguity.

## User-story driven documentation

Every page answers one user story. Write it before you start and use it to cut anything that doesn't move the reader toward that goal.

```text
As a developer, I want to fetch all records from a paginated API
so that I can process them without missing any data.
```

The title serves the story. The introduction sets it up. Every step moves toward completing it. If a section doesn't serve the story, cut it.

## Storytelling structure for introductions

Three steps for setting up any how-to guide or tutorial introduction.

1. **Start where the user is.** Meet your reader at their current level of understanding. Don't assume context they don't have yet.
2. **Break the problem into logical sections.** Structure the content so each section builds on the previous one.
3. **Eliminate everything that doesn't help the reader solve their problem.** If a sentence doesn't move the reader forward, remove it.

## Microsoft voice principles

| Principle | What it means |
|---|---|
| **Warm and relaxed** | Natural, less formal, grounded in real conversations |
| **Crisp and clear** | Write for scanning first, reading second |
| **Ready to help** | Anticipate real needs and offer the right information at the right time |
| **Get to the point fast** | Start with the key takeaway. Put the most important thing in the most noticeable spot. |
| **Use sentence case** | Less formal than title case, reflects modern style |
