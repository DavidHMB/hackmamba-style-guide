Your job is not to become a technical expert. Your job is to represent the reader. SMEs respect writers who ask the right questions, not writers who pretend to know what they don't.

## Eight principles for working with SMEs

<Steps>
  <Step title="Leave your ego at the door">
    Don't try to impress SMEs with their own terminology. The only people you need to worry about are your readers. If an SME looks down on you for not knowing the jargon, you're thinking like your reader correctly.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Don't embrace the jargon">
    Understand technical terms, then decide if they help the reader. If customers use the term comfortably, keep it. If they don't, cut it. If in doubt, explain the term once at first use.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Don't waste the SME's time">
    Prepare questions before every meeting. Think through the problems users might face with the feature. Check whether a colleague has already asked the SME about something you need.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Respect the SME's workload">
    If an SME says they're too busy, explain your deadline calmly. If neither of you can resolve the conflict, escalate to management.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Arrive calm and on time">
    You may receive dismissive responses when you ask basic questions. If you arrive stressed, you risk being confrontational without meaning to be.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Admit when you don't understand">
    Never nod along when you're lost. A few minutes of feeling awkward is far better than publishing incorrect documentation.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Never lose your temper">
    If an SME makes your struggle public, laugh it off. Staying calm protects the relationship.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Be judged on your output">
    When your documentation helps customers and reduces support queries, people will respect what you do.
  </Step>
</Steps>

## Source credibility by role

Different roles give you different layers of truth. Combine them all.

| Role | What they know |
|---|---|
| Developers | How the product actually works |
| Support teams | What breaks and what confuses customers |
| Product management | Strategy and positioning |
| Pre-sales and marketing | Customer stories and pain points |

## Interview technique

- Prepare 20 questions. Expect to use five to ten.
- Open with: "What do you want people to know about this?" and let them talk for 20 minutes.
- Record everything. Take notes alongside any recording. Recordings fail.
- Clarify goals before you start writing: "Is there anything you really want users to know?"
- Never ask the same question twice.

## After the interview

Organize your notes by topic, not by the order questions were asked. Extract the facts that users need. Separate what the SME told you from what you still need to verify against the live product.
